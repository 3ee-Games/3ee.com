A Deep Dive into Stable Diffusion Samplers

In the captivating world of generative art and Stable Diffusion (SD), samplers play a role in shaping intricate images by navigating the terrain of high-dimensional differential equations. At the heart of SD lies a complex differential equation that dictates the diffusion of initial noise across an image to minimize a loss function. This function represents the distance to an ideal image conceived from a seed and a prompt. Encoded within the vast neural network weights of a Stable Diffusion model are the solutions to this intricate equation, manifested as billion-plus floating-point numbers.

The Sampler‚Äôs Dance

Conceptually, it operates by sampling the latent space, calculating local gradients at each step, and determining the direction for the next move. Similar to a ball rolling down a multidimensional hill, the sampler aims to descend as low as possible to minimize the loss function. However, what appears locally as the fastest route may not always lead to an optimal solution; samplers might find themselves caught in local optima, necessitating occasional ascents to discover a superior path downward. The complexity of this process is heightened by the vast multidimensional space in which it operates.

Samplers in Stable Diffusion are different methods of solving differential equations.

Leonhard Euler‚Äôs method, dating back to the 1700s, stands as the original of solving differential equations. Despite its simplicity and quick computation, it accumulates errors rapidly unless a substantial number of steps (small step size) is employed. Surprisingly, Euler‚Äôs method finds compatibility with SD, excelling in the pursuit of local optimality rather than accurate approximation.

Heun‚Äôs method improves upon Euler by introducing a correction step to reduce errors. However, it operates at roughly twice the speed of Euler, making it less appealing in practice.

LMS (Linear Multi-Step)

üîó Wikipedia - Linear Multistep Method

LMS takes a leap forward by incorporating information from several prior steps to predict the next sample more accurately.

An intriguing variant, Pseudo-Numerical methods for Diffusion Models (PLMS), emerges as a version of LMS tailored for Diffusion Models.

DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models)

üîó ArXiv - Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models üìù Jiaming Song, Chenlin Meng, Stefano Ermon

As one of the original samplers bundled with SD, DDIM introduces Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models. It demands a higher number of steps compared to its more recent counterparts.

DPM (Diffusion Probabilistic Model)

üîó ArXiv - Diffusion Probabilistic Models üìù Cheng Lu, Yuhao Zhou, Fan Bao, Jianfei Chen, Chongxuan Li, Jun Zhu

A dedicated solver crafted for diffusion differential equations, DPM, emerged in June 2022

DPM++

üîó ArXiv - DPM++ üìù Cheng Lu, Yuhao Zhou, Fan Bao, Jianfei Chen, Chongxuan Li, Jun Zhu

An enhanced version of DPM, DPM++ boasts improved results, especially at high guidance (CFG) values.

DPM++ 2M and 2S

üîó ArXiv - DPM++ 2M and 2S üìù Cheng Lu, Yuhao Zhou, Fan Bao, Jianfei Chen, Chongxuan Li, Jun Zhu

Variants of DPM++, DPM++ 2M and 2S, incorporate second-order derivatives, offering slower yet more accurate performance. DPM++ 2M (Karras) stands out as one of the top-performing samplers in terms of speed and quality.

DPM++ 3M

üîó ArXiv - DPM++ 3M üìù Cheng Lu, Yuhao Zhou, Fan Bao, Jianfei Chen, Chongxuan Li, Jun Zhu

Taking a step further, DPM++ 3M employs third-order derivatives, promising even greater accuracy at the expense of speed.

üîó ArXiv - Unified Predictor‚ÄìCorrector Framework üìù Wenliang Zhao, Lujia Bai, Yongming Rao, Jie Zhou, Jiwen Lu

UniPC is a Unified Predictor‚ÄìCorrector Framework, quick to converge and yielding promising results. Notably, its ‚Äúcorrector‚Äù component (UniC) is versatile, potentially compatible with other sampler types.

Restart

üîó ArXiv - Restart üìù Yilun Xu, Mingyang Deng, Xiang Cheng, Yonglong Tian, Ziming Liu, Tommi Jaakkola

A novel algorithm by Yilun Xu et al., Restart introduces periodic ‚Äúrestarts‚Äù by adding noise between normal noise reduction steps. Claimed to combine the advantages of both deterministic and stochastic samplers, it offers speed while avoiding local optima.

An ‚Äúancestral‚Äù variant, leveraging probability distributions and conditional probabilities to introduce stochasticity. These variants diverge from traditional solutions, often producing more creative outcomes.

SDE (Stochastic Differential Equations)

üîó ArXiv - Exponential üìù Test

Incorporating Stochastic Differential Equations, these variants introduce random drift to explore potential solutions in a non-deterministic manner, similar to ancestral samplers. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic_differential_equation

In an interesting aside, the DPM++ 2M SDE, when paired with a low eta and the Heun solver type (referred to as DPM++ 2M SDE Heun Exponential in A1111), has proven to be a noteworthy solution. This configuration, as outlined in the RES (Refined Exponential Solver) Paper and subsequently implemented by Birchlabs, maximizes sampling quality and serves as an effective strategy for enhancing overall image quality.

Best one to use?

Selecting the ‚Äúbest‚Äù sampler is inherently subjective, as it hinges on individual preferences and the desired visual outcome. However, when it comes to achieving a specific look, certain samplers may stand out.

From a technical standpoint, anecdotal insights, often derived from Stability staff observations and collective comments, suggest that Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) samplers tend to offer superior perceptual results‚Äîa sentiment I find myself aligning with.

In my exploration of customized samplers, I‚Äôve noticed that SDE variants, such as Brownian Tree, excel in capturing perceptual details, especially in intricate features like minute skin nuances or other high-frequency information. This enhanced detail is attributed to the interval and rate at which noise is sampled during the diffusion process.

For images requiring a softer, flatter, or less detailed aesthetic, the Euler sampler has proven to be a preferred choice in my experience. It consistently produces outputs with a softer touch, particularly at the same number of steps when compared to other single-step samplers.

On the other hand, when dealing with more detailed images, my preference leans toward any of the DPM++ SDE variants. Among them, the 2M SDE variant has stood the test of time, demonstrating reliability and thorough testing.

In conclusion, the quest for the ideal sampler is a nuanced journey, intertwined with artistic preferences and the visual intricacies of the desired outcome. Understanding the strengths of each sampler in various contexts empowers creators to wield these tools strategically, unlocking the full potential of generative art in the realm of Stable Diffusion.
